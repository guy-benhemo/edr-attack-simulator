# Guardz Product & Service Content

## Service Description

Guardz is a holistic cybersecurity platform that provides enterprise-grade cyber technology
and makes it accessible to SMB/SMEs. We empower MSPs and IT professionals to secure small
businesses inside out from common threats such as phishing, ransomware/malware, data loss,
user risk, etc. in a single unified solution.

## Security Modules

### Compromised Data (Dark Web Monitoring)
Tracks leaked credentials found in data dumps and lists on the dark web. Guardz scrapes
and analyzes sources that monitor the dark web for malicious activity targeting businesses
and users. Analyzes domains related to the company and surfaces leaked credentials as
high-severity issues.

### External Surface
Performs vulnerability assessments analyzing networks, servers, and endpoints to identify
weaknesses — open ports, misconfigurations, and unpatched software. Provides comprehensive
vulnerability reports to help businesses prioritize and address security risks.

### Cloud Data Security
Monitors and protects cloud environments with real-time visibility into cloud application
security. Detects misconfigurations, monitors user access privileges, discovers externally
exposed data, and identifies potential threats to safeguard integrity and confidentiality
of cloud data.

### Cybersecurity Awareness & Phishing Simulations
Comprehensive awareness training programs educating employees about cyber threats including
phishing attacks, social engineering techniques, and password security. AI-driven Phishing
Simulations provide an adaptable and flexible approach to testing user awareness and
identifying education gaps.

### Web Browsing Protection
Blocks access to malicious sites, monitors downloads for potential threats, and provides
real-time threat intelligence. Secures the browsing experience against web-based attacks
that could compromise systems and data.

### Device Protection (Endpoints)
Advanced antivirus and anti-malware solutions with real-time scanning, behavior-based
analysis, and automatic threat response mechanisms to detect and mitigate malicious
activities on devices. Prevents malware infections and unauthorized access attempts.

### Email Security
Robust filters, anti-spam measures, and email quarantine leveraging machine learning
algorithms to identify and block suspicious emails. Reduces the risk of successful
email-based attacks including phishing, ransomware, and business email compromise.

## Brand Voice & Persona

- **Who is writing:** A senior security researcher / practitioner who works in the trenches daily. NOT a marketer. The author has "battle scars" and talks like someone who has debugged real incidents at 2 AM.
- **Who is reading:** MSPs (Managed Service Providers) and their technical staff who protect SMB (Small and Medium Business) clients. Assume the reader knows what Entra ID, MFA, and EDR are — but explain novel attack chains in depth.
- **Tone:** Confident, direct, slightly informal, occasionally blunt. Think "experienced colleague briefing you over coffee," not "vendor whitepaper." Allowed to have personality and opinions.

## Tone Rules

| DO                                          | DON'T                                      |
|---------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Write in first person plural ("we")         | Use stiff corporate third-person            |
| State opinions plainly ("That made sense in 2015. It doesn't make sense in 2025.") | Hedge with weak qualifiers                  |
| Use short, punchy sentences for emphasis     | Write long, winding academic paragraphs     |
| Address the reader directly ("your customers," "your inbox") | Stay abstract and impersonal               |
| Use colloquial phrasing ("you are cooked," "the agent already runs code") | Sound like a press release                  |
| Be candid about industry gaps ("Most SOC teams filter on successful authentications. I cannot overstate how common this blind spot is.") | Pretend everything is fine                  |
| Inject urgency without hype                  | Use fear-mongering clickbait language        |

## Structural Patterns

### Opening Hook
- Start with a **breaking event, bold claim, or provocative question** — never a generic intro.
- Examples of strong openers:
  - "Microsoft dropped research yesterday that confirms what a few of us have been nervously sketching on whiteboards…"
  - "Should you outsource your security operations center? That's a burning question MSPs are being forced to ask."
  - "Agentic AI is changing the rules of the game."
- First paragraph should make the reader feel *behind* if they stop reading.

### Body Structure
- Use **H2 / H3 headers** liberally — readers scan.
- Alternate between **narrative explanation** and **technical deep-dives** (code blocks, parameter breakdowns, log examples).
- Include **real telemetry, real error codes, real CLI output** wherever possible.
- Tables are used for structured comparisons and settings breakdowns.
- Bullet points are acceptable for lists of features, stats, or action items — but always wrapped in narrative context.

### Closing / CTA
- End with a **practical takeaway or call to action** tied to Guardz capabilities.
- Reinforce that Guardz is where the detection/research happened — "This matters because at Guardz, the MSPs and SMBs we protect are exactly the organizations that get wrecked by this class of attack."
- Common CTA phrasing: "Book a demo," "Get started," "Join the conversation" (links to LinkedIn discussions).

## Formatting Conventions

- **Bold** for key terms on first mention, setting names, and emphasis phrases.
- *Italics* for parameter values, file names, and editorial asides.
- `Code blocks` for URLs, CLI commands, error codes, file paths, and OAuth parameters.
- **Horizontal rules (---)** to separate major sections.
- **Images/diagrams** with descriptive alt text — flowcharts of attack chains, screenshots of admin consoles, detection timelines.
- **"Key Takeaways" or "Key takeaways"** section near the top of educational/guide posts (bullet format).
- **"Note:"** or **"Tip:"** inline callouts for practical MSP advice.
- Series labeling: "This article is part of the 'How-to guide for MSPs' series."

## Sentence-Level Patterns

1. **Short declarative punch after a setup:** "The attacker does not want a token. They want the redirect."
2. **Rhetorical "Here's the thing" framing:** "And here is the part that should bother everyone."
3. **Contrast structure:** "[Old thing] made sense in [year]. It doesn't make sense in [current year]."
4. **Direct reader callout:** "Are you still downloading skills from public libraries? Because the moment you trust an unknown library, you are cooked."
5. **Evidence-backed opinion:** "I cannot overstate how common this blind spot is." / "I have not tested whether that is consistently true, but…"
6. **Guardz self-reference as proof point:** "Over the past two months, the Guardz ITDR and Research team has been tracking [X]…" / "Last year, we investigated a customer environment and found…"
7. **Statistics as anchors:** Lead paragraphs with a hard stat, then explain its implications. ("Nearly 50% of U.S. small businesses have been hit by a cyberattack.")

## What NOT to Do

- Don't write like a generic cybersecurity vendor blog (vague, buzzword-heavy, no real evidence).
- Don't sanitize the language to the point of blandness — keep the practitioner edge.
- Don't assume the reader needs basic cybersecurity definitions (they're MSPs).
- Don't use "cutting-edge," "next-generation," "robust," or "seamless" — these are empty.
- Don't end with a generic "Stay safe out there!" — end with a specific action or insight.
- Don't write without at least one concrete data point, real-world example, or detection artifact.
